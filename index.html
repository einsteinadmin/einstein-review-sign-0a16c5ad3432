The order, in one breath: 18×24" vertical, 4mm coroplast, double-sided, matte finish. Files include the standard 0.125" bleed (pages are 18.25×24.25") — every sign shop will know exactly what to do with them. Options worth the couple bucks: rounded corners (die-cut at order time — reads friendly, not yard-sale) and dry-erase lamination on the Mad Lib face + a wet-erase marker clipped to the board. Pair designs back-to-back (e.g. Flagship / 10-10 on one board) so 2–3 boards = 4–6 designs on the truck. Runs ~$10–15/board at qty 10 from a local shop (SpeedPro, FASTSIGNS) or BuildASign online. Fair Cutout special instruction: tell the shop to die-cut along the outer edge of the magenta ring — the whole ring is removed (it's a cut marker, not ink), leaving an ~8×10" oval face hole with the hair and mustache bordering it. Logo: the real Einstein lockup is on every file already — nothing to swap, nothing to add.

Production notes for Austin

  • Size & stock: 18×24" vertical, 4mm coroplast (light, lives in a truck cab, survives rain). Rounded corners so it reads friendly, not yard-sign.
  • Two-sided: print a different concept on each face — 2 boards = 4 designs. At ~$10–15/board, carry 2–3 per truck and let the customer flip through and pick their favorite — the choosing is part of the fun.
  • #3 needs dry-erase laminate + a wet-erase marker clipped to the board (test marker ghosting on a proof first).
  • Real logo on every design — kept small on purpose; the photo is the customer's moment, not an ad unit.
  • Watch (Cameron, 7/2): the Einstein character himself only appears tiny inside the logo lockup. Probably fine — these photos live next to reviews where context does the work — but if the beta photos feel under-branded, the tweak is the Einstein head icon as a corner accent or a bigger lockup.
  • Contrast check outdoors: all pairings here are WCAG-approved kit combos, which conveniently also means they survive harsh sunlight and phone-camera compression.

Getting the shot — keep it a 20-second moment

  • When: at the final walkthrough, right when the relief kicks in. "Want a fun photo with the crew? Takes 20 seconds, totally optional."
  • The customer picks the sign. Fan out the 2–3 boards and let them choose — the choosing is half the fun and it never feels scripted.
  • Where: wherever the day happened. Front porch, in front of the truck (free branding in the frame), the empty living room. Don't stage it — real beats perfect.
  • Who: customer + crew is the money shot. Customer solo or selfie-style with the movers both work great. Kids only with a parent's explicit OK.
  • Who shoots: a mover, on the CUSTOMER's phone. It's their photo to keep and post. If they say yes to sharing, snap a second one on the crew phone for our socials.
  • After the crew rolls out, we text them the review link — separate moment, no pressure, their call.

Field rules — how crews use it (the part that keeps us safe)

  • Photo and review are two separate asks. The sign photo is a fun keepsake + social content. Never "hold this for your Google review."
  • The review ask is universal. Every customer, every job — the competitor's "ask the happy ones" version is literally the violation (review gating). The photo can be offered to whoever's game; the review ask can't be selective.
  • Review link gets texted after the crew leaves. Google's 2026 policy bans pressuring reviews on-premises, and posting from a crew device or shared Wi-Fi trips their filters. We hand them the photo; posting it with their review is their call, their phone, their time.
  • No incentives, ever. Nothing free, no discount, no raffle tied to a review or the photo — and no per-review bonuses for crews. (FTC rule, fines up to ~$53K per violation.)
  • Never say "5-star" and never ask them to mention a mover by name in the review — both are now explicit Google violations. "We'd love an honest review" is the whole script.
  • Documented consent before socials: a saved "reply YES" text or one-tap OK in EMMA before any photo hits our pages (Florida's likeness statute has punitive damages; Texas has the same tort). No kids without a parent's explicit yes.
  • Yelp is never mentioned. Their policy bans asking entirely.
